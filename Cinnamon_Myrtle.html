Overview

The cinnamon myrtle which is also known by its traditional name Wee-la-la among some Aboriginal people and it is a small tree that is native to the subtropical rainforests of Eastern Australia. It grows up to 7 metres tall and forms a low canopy of glossy green leaves that are 3 to 6 centimetres in size with a spicy cinnamon aroma when crushed and it produces star-shaped flowers that are cream-coloured. Although it is not widely consumed, the fruit of the cinnamon myrtle is a small, woody capsule containing miniscule seeds and the leaves are highly valued for their culinary and medicinal uses

Where it grows
The cinnamon myrtle which is also known by its traditional name Wee-la-la among some Aboriginal people and it is a small tree that is native to the subtropical rainforests of Eastern Australia. It grows up to 7 metres tall and forms a low canopy of glossy green leaves that are 3 to 6 centimetres in size with a spicy cinnamon aroma when crushed and it produces star-shaped flowers that are cream-coloured. Although it is not widely consumed, the fruit of the cinnamon myrtle is a small, woody capsule containing miniscule seeds and the leaves are highly valued for their culinary and medicinal uses

Nutritional value identified by First Nations People
The First Nations people have long recognised the nutritional value of the cinnamon myrtle and they have traditionally used their leaves for culinary and medicinal purposes. Even though they did not do specific nutritional analyses, Indigenous people understood the benefits of consuming it and they ate the fruit of it fresh or dried and also used it to flavour food. The leaves have essential oils such as eucalyptol, limonene and terpinene and are known for their antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties and it contains a range of essential nutrients is a good source of fibre. It can also help with digestive issues such as heartburn, colic and dyspepsia and can provide respiratory relief and support your immune system.

How we use (your fruit) today
Cinnamon myrtle continues to be used in traditional medicine and it is now popular for it to be used in modern cuisines as well. The leaves are used as flavouring in teas, desserts and savoury foods. The essential oils that are extracted from it are also used in aromatherapy, perfumes and cleaning products.

Why we should farm (your fruit)
Farming cinnamon myrtle offers several advantages for Australian farmers. There is economic opportunity for the farmers as the cinnamon myrtle is a versatile plant that can be used for several commercial applications such as essential oil production, culinary use and woodworking. By farming it, farmers can generate additional income due to the demand for this plant as there is a growing popularity for native Australian food and ingredients and its health benefits. It is a pretty low maintenance and adaptable crop that can be grown sustainably and is suited to Australian weather conditions and can be easily integrated into farming systems. The cinnamon myrtle can also provide habitat for Australian Native wildlife such as birds and small mammals.
